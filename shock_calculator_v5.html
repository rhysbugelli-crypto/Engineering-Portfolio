C–D Nozzle Flow Regimes
How nozzle flow changes as ambient back pressure pₐ/p₀ is lowered

The flow through a converging-diverging nozzle is governed by the ratio of ambient pressure to inlet stagnation pressure, pa/p0. As this ratio is progressively lowered, the nozzle passes through eight distinct flow regimes. Note that pa is the pressure of the surrounding environment the nozzle exhausts into — it is not the exit plane pressure of the gas itself, except in certain regimes where they happen to be equal.

Three critical pressure ratios define the boundaries between regimes:

pap0sub
Choking onset — the throat first reaches M = 1
pap0NS
Normal shock sits exactly at the nozzle exit
pap0sup
Design condition — perfectly expanded supersonic flow

Ordering:  1 > pap0sub  >  pap0NS  >  pap0sup  > 0

1
No Flow
pap0 ≥ 1

When the ambient pressure equals or exceeds the stagnation pressure, there is no pressure gradient to drive flow. The gas is stationary throughout the nozzle.

[ Mach & pressure distribution diagrams — coming soon ]
2
Isentropic Subsonic Flow (Not Choked)
pap0sub < pap0 < 1

Lowering pa below p0 creates a pressure gradient that drives subsonic flow. The Mach number increases through the converging section, reaches a local maximum at the throat (M < 1), then decreases through the diverging section — which acts as a subsonic diffuser. The flow is entirely isentropic and the exit pressure matches ambient: pe = pa.

[ Mach & pressure distribution diagrams — coming soon ]
3
Choked Subsonic Flow
pap0 = pap0sub

The pressure ratio is just low enough to choke the flow at the throat: M = 1 there. The flow accelerates to sonic conditions at the throat, then decelerates subsonically through the diverging section. The flow is still entirely isentropic and pe = pa.

From this point onward, further reductions in pa/p0 do not change anything upstream of the throat. The converging section and throat are locked — all subsequent changes occur in the diverging section or beyond the exit plane.

Internal Shock Regimes
Throat is choked · normal shock exists inside or at the exit plane.
4
Normal Shock Inside the Nozzle
pap0NS < pap0 < pap0sub

The throat remains choked (M = 1). Flow accelerates to supersonic conditions in the diverging section, but the ambient pressure is still too high to support supersonic flow all the way to the exit. A normal shock forms inside the diverging section to bridge the pressure gap, returning the flow to subsonic before the exit, where pe = pa.

As pa/p0 is lowered within this range, the shock moves progressively downstream — it first appears at the throat and migrates toward the exit plane as pa/p0 approaches (pa/p0)NS.

[ Mach & pressure distribution diagrams — coming soon ]
5
Normal Shock at the Nozzle Exit
pap0 = pap0NS

The limiting case of Regime 4. The normal shock has moved all the way to the exit plane. The flow inside the nozzle is now fully supersonic through the entire diverging section, with a normal shock standing exactly at the exit to jump the pressure up to pa.

This is the last regime in which the internal nozzle flow is affected by changes in pa. Below this ratio, the flow field inside the nozzle is fixed.

External Adjustment Regimes
Fully supersonic internal flow · jet adjusts to ambient pressure outside the nozzle.
6
Overexpanded Jet
pap0sup < pap0 < pap0NS

The internal flow is now fully supersonic and isentropic to the exit — identical to the design condition — but pe < pa. The gas has been expanded too far relative to what the ambient pressure requires (hence overexpanded). The pressure adjustment now occurs outside the nozzle.

Since pa is below the normal-shock threshold, a normal shock would overshoot the required pressure rise. Instead, weaker oblique shocks form just outside the nozzle exit in a characteristic diamond pattern. As pa/p0 is lowered toward (pa/p0)sup, these oblique shocks become progressively weaker.

[ Mach & pressure distribution diagrams — coming soon ]
7
Perfectly Expanded — Design Condition
pap0 = pap0sup

The nozzle operates at its design point. The flow is fully supersonic and isentropic throughout, and the exit pressure exactly matches ambient: pe = pa. No shocks or expansion waves form outside the nozzle. This is the optimal operating condition.

[ Mach & pressure distribution diagrams — coming soon ]
8
Underexpanded Jet
pap0 < pap0sup

The ambient pressure has dropped below the design value, so pe > pa — the gas has not been expanded far enough (hence underexpanded). The internal flow and exit conditions are identical to the design point, since further reductions in pa cannot affect anything upstream of the exit plane.

The excess pressure is relieved outside the nozzle through Prandtl–Meyer expansion fans, which form at the nozzle lip and turn the flow outward, continuing to accelerate and expand the gas beyond the exit Mach number.
